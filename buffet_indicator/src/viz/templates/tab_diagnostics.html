Diagnostics — Statistical Health

Power-user view of the underlying statistical machinery: stationarity tests, Bai-Perron structural breaks, cross-variant correlations, OOS R² evolution, residual autocorrelation, and probability-engine calibration. Use these to gauge whether the headline signals are robust or whether one or more constituent indicators is drifting away from its historical regime.

1. Stationarity tests (per variant, long-run frame)

Four complementary unit-root / stationarity tests run on each variant's long-run z-score series. ADF (null: unit root) and PP (Phillips-Perron, null: unit root) reject when p < 0.05. KPSS (null: stationarity) fails to reject when p > 0.05. ZA (Zivot-Andrews, null: unit root with no structural break) rejects when p < 0.05. Overall PASS iff at least 2 of the 4 tests agree on stationarity — agreement strengthens the signal because each test has a different power profile against different alternatives.

2. Cross-variant z-score correlation matrix

Pearson correlations between the long-run z-score series of every variant, computed over the common observation window. High off-diagonal values indicate redundancy; low values indicate the variants pick up independent valuation signals.

MVCI PCA first-PC explained variance: {{ pc1_explained_fmt }}. The PC1 fraction quantifies how much of the joint variation is captured by a single latent factor; values above 60% support the use of a composite index.

3. Bai-Perron structural break dates (per variant)

Bai-Perron multiple-break detection identifies structural breaks endogenously under a piecewise-linear-in-time model. Up to 5 breaks per variant, selected by BIC. The 95% CI uses an asymptotic ±18-month band as a conservative proxy for the proper Bai-Perron interval.

4. Out-of-sample R² evolution (MVCI 10Y, expanding window)

Goyal-Welch R2OOS computed at each month-end on an expanding window: at each date t, the model is fit on data through t-1 and used to predict t. Positive values mean the predictive model outperforms a no-skill mean forecast on the next observation; persistently positive values across decades support the predictive use of the indicator.

5. Residual autocorrelation (MVCI 10Y predictive regression)

Sample autocorrelation (ACF) and partial autocorrelation (PACF) of the OLS residuals from the MVCI z → 10Y forward CAGR regression, 20 lags. Dashed red bands are the 95% confidence interval under the null of white noise (Bartlett ±1.96/√N). Lags spiking outside the band indicate omitted dynamics — typically the overlapping-observation effect on the 10Y horizon, which the Newey-West HAC SE on the headline already controls for.

7. Caveats

  • All test statistics are sample estimates and are themselves subject to sampling variability — interpret p-values as continuous evidence rather than as binary accept/reject decisions.
  • The 10Y forward-return target induces overlapping observations; the Newey-West t-stat with lag = horizon controls for this, but the effective sample size is much smaller than the raw observation count would suggest.
  • The Mean Reversion variant uses a log-linear trend fit over the full sample — by construction the residuals have zero mean over the sample, which is not the same as long-run zero mean for future data.
  • The MVCI composite z-score is constructed equal-weighted by default (most robust); the PCA-PC1 and inverse-variance schemes are reported as sensitivity checks in the MVCI tab's "Weighting Schemes" table.
  • Bai-Perron CIs above use a conservative ±18-month asymptotic band. The proper Bai-Perron interval depends on within-segment variance; the band here is a documentation device, not a hypothesis test.
  • Calibration uses recursive Gaussian probability estimates (no look-ahead). The headline tab reports Bayesian posterior probabilities which include a weakly-informative prior; calibration here measures the OLS-only estimate.
