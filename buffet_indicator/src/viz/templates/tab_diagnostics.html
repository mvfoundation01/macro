Diagnostics — Statistical Health

Power-user view of the underlying statistical machinery: stationarity tests, cross-variant correlations, predictive R² properties, and PCA explained variance. Use these to gauge whether the headline signals are robust or whether one or more constituent indicators is drifting away from its historical regime.

1. Stationarity tests (per variant, long-run frame)

ADF tests the null of a unit root (alternative: stationary). KPSS tests the null of stationarity. A variant z-score series passes the stationarity check iff ADF rejects (p < 0.05) and KPSS fails to reject (p > 0.05).

Interpretation: a FAIL on ADF or KPSS does not necessarily invalidate a variant — many valuation series exhibit structural breaks (regime changes in capital intensity, buyback adoption, free cash flow margins) that legitimately violate stationarity. Use this as a screen for "this variant's distribution may be drifting" rather than a reject/accept decision rule.

2. Cross-variant z-score correlation matrix

Pearson correlations between the long-run z-score series of every variant, computed over the common observation window. High off-diagonal values indicate redundancy; low values indicate the variants pick up independent valuation signals.

MVCI PCA first-PC explained variance: {{ pc1_explained_fmt }}. The PC1 fraction quantifies how much of the joint variation is captured by a single latent factor; values above 60% support the use of a composite index.

3. Out-of-sample R² evolution (MVCI 10Y, expanding window)

Goyal-Welch R2OOS computed at each month-end on an expanding window: at each date t, the model is fit on data through t-1 and used to predict t. Positive values mean the predictive model outperforms a no-skill mean forecast on the next observation; persistently positive values across decades support the predictive use of the indicator.

4. Predictive properties — key metrics

Newey-West t-statistics, in-sample R2, and out-of-sample R2 (Goyal-Welch) are reported per-variant on the dedicated indicator tabs. The relevant headline summary is in the right rail of each tab's "Predictive Regression" block.

Goyal-Welch R2OOS compares a recursively-fit model against a no-skill mean prediction; positive values indicate genuine out-of-sample forecasting skill. Negative R2OOS means the recursive model underperforms the unconditional mean — a strong red flag against using the variant for forecasting.

5. Probability engine calibration

The Bayesian posterior CIs reported on each variant tab are constructed from a conjugate normal model with weakly informative priors. Calibration metrics (Brier score decomposition, PIT histograms, reliability diagrams) are computed during the orchestrator's backtest pass and saved to outputs/tables/calibration_metrics.json. Use the Data tab to download those tables for offline analysis.

6. Caveats

  • All test statistics are sample estimates and are themselves subject to sampling variability — interpret p-values as continuous evidence rather than as binary accept/reject decisions.
  • The 10Y forward-return target induces overlapping observations; the Newey-West t-stat with lag = horizon controls for this, but the effective sample size is much smaller than the raw observation count would suggest.
  • The Mean Reversion variant uses a log-linear trend fit over the full sample — by construction the residuals have zero mean over the sample, which is not the same as long-run zero mean for future data.
  • The MVCI composite z-score is constructed equal-weighted by default (most robust); the PCA-PC1 and inverse-variance schemes are reported as sensitivity checks in the MVCI tab's "Weighting Schemes" table.
